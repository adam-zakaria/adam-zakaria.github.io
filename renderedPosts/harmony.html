-> And competing species arise through natural genetic variation?

<- Yes, competing species arise through natural genetic variation, which is a fundamental mechanism of evolution. Here's how it works:

  1. Genetic Variation: Genetic variation occurs naturally within a population due to mutations, gene shuffling during sexual reproduction, and other processes. This variation is the raw material for evolution.
  2. Natural Selection: Individuals with traits that provide a survival or reproductive advantage are more likely to pass those traits on to the next generation. Over time, these advantageous traits become more common in the population. Speciation: When populations of a species become isolated (geographically, ecologically, or behaviorally), they may evolve independently. Over time, genetic differences can accumulate, leading to the emergence of new species. This process is known as speciation.
  3. Competition: As new species arise, they may compete with existing species for resources such as food, space, and mates. This competition can drive further evolutionary changes, as species adapt to their environments and to each other. Overall, genetic variation and natural selection are key drivers of biodiversity and the emergence of competing species.
